01 — About

Background

I am a Master's student in Computational Neuroscience at the Bernstein Center for Computational Neuroscience (BCCN), Berlin, with a background in computer science, machine learning, and software engineering.

My research interests lie at the intersection of cognitive neuroscience, artificial intelligence, and computational modeling. I am broadly interested in how brain activity gives rise to cognitive abilities such as perception, attention, memory, decision-making, and cognitive control, and how these abilities can be studied through computational and data-driven models.

In my current work as a Student Assistant at the Science of Intelligence Excellence Cluster (SCIoI, TU Berlin), I investigate human gaze behavior in dynamic scenes by integrating vision-language models, large multimodal models, and eye-tracking data. This project allows me to study how structured representations of the visual world relate to attention and behavior.

During my lab rotation at the Neuroimaging Group at BCCN Berlin, I worked with fMRI data and multivariate pattern analysis to examine how experimental conditions can be decoded from patterns of brain activity. This experience strengthened my interest in linking computational models with neural data.

More broadly, I am interested in developing models that help explain how biological intelligence represents information, selects relevant features, and supports flexible cognition.

Scene Graph Generation & Human Visual Attention
↗ SCIoI Project 57

Research question: Which semantic relation clusters in an open-vocabulary scene graph are preferentially selected by human gaze in dynamic environments?

This project investigates the link between structured scene representations and human visual attention. The full pipeline constructs open-vocabulary scene graphs from dynamic videos, extracts gaze-based temporal relations from eye-tracking scanpaths, and compares which semantic relation types humans preferentially attend to — distinguishing between relations that are merely frequent in the environment versus those actively selected by attention.

STEP 04
Attention Analysis
Compare scene-wide relation distribution vs. gaze-selected relations to identify attentional preference

fMRI Experimental Design Evaluation — NeuroImaging Rotation
BCCN Berlin

Goal: Evaluate the validity and decoding reliability of an ongoing lab experiment using real fMRI data.

As part of my lab rotation at the NeuroImaging Group (BCCN Berlin), I worked with real fMRI data from an active research project. I applied the Same Analysis Approach (SAA) to assess whether the experimental design could support robust, reproducible decoding — a principled method that tests whether the same analytical pipeline succeeds on real data but not on permuted controls, directly probing design validity before drawing scientific conclusions.

Decoding was carried out using The Decoding Toolbox (TDT) within the SPM framework, applying multivariate pattern analysis (MVPA) to identify brain regions whose activation patterns reliably distinguish experimental conditions. As a final deliverable, I produced a step-by-step tutorial covering the full SAA–TDT workflow to help the lab run reproducible analyses in future projects.

Customer Satisfaction Prediction with Michigan-Style LCS
↗ Published Paper

Problem: Predicting bank customer satisfaction from a high-dimensional, severely class-imbalanced dataset (96% satisfied vs. 4% unsatisfied) using an interpretable rule-based learning system.

Applied a Michigan-style Learning Classifier System (LCS) — a genetic-based machine learning approach that evolves a population of IF:THEN rules — to classify Santander Bank customers. The raw dataset contained 369 anonymized features across 147,392 records. A rigorous preprocessing pipeline handled class imbalance via SMOTE, removed constant and highly-correlated features (threshold: 0.85), and converted all attributes to binary format for rule-based processing.

Three rule compaction strategies were benchmarked — QRC, QRF, and PDRC — using 5-fold cross-validation. QRC achieved the highest accuracy (88.4%), while QRF was the fastest. Attribute Tracking and Feedback mechanisms further identified the four most predictive features driving customer dissatisfaction.

Human Resource Allocation via Process Mining & TOC
↗ Published Paper

Problem: In banking, inefficient human resource allocation across credit requirement workflows leads to process bottlenecks, increased costs, and reduced client satisfaction — yet few works had combined process mining with bottleneck resolution techniques.

This study proposes a four-step methodology to optimise human resource allocation in the credit requirement process of a real banking system. First, the Heuristic Miner algorithm (via ProM 6.9) discovers the actual workflow from event logs — a seven-stage process spanning 10,035 instances. Second, statistical analysis (MLE and Method of Moments estimation, Cullen & Frey graphs, Kolmogorov–Smirnov and Anderson–Darling tests) identifies the probability distribution of each activity's execution time.

Third, a Differential Evolution (DE) algorithm — an evolutionary optimiser using mutation, crossover, and greedy selection — simulates four scenarios under a LIFO queue discipline to find the optimal resource allocation per activity, outperforming prior approaches (Genetic Algorithm, IDE) by converging to the optimal solution in fewer iterations. Fourth, the identified bottleneck ("Collection of Documents") is resolved using the Theory of Constraints (TOC) thinking process — Current and Future Reality Trees trace root causes (untrained staff, lack of digital infrastructure) and inject targeted solutions including a proposed online document collection architecture.
